Section 2. E-portfolio and professional development

Introduction

In this section we are going to look at the importance of building an e-portfolio for professional development and the theoretical background behind it. We’ll also look at some of the practicalities of making an e-portfolio.

Objectives

The objectives of this section are to raise awareness of:

  • Professional development in our field.
  • The construction of a portfolio for professional development.
  • The organization of contents in your portfolio.

Content

  • What is a professional development?
  • Why and e-portfolio?
  • Background theory supporting e-portfolios.
  • What competencies can be developed with an e-portfolio?
  • Organizing your e-portfolio.

What is professional development?

As we learnt in FOLC, people working in a profession like teaching may have expectations about their own developments. Professional development is a continuous and on-going processes which should help teachers to turn problems they face in their professional careers into positive, rather than negative, experiences. Professional development is also an opportunity to build upon what you know, as a teacher, and to try out new methods and approaches in the classroom. For a more detailed reminder of what professional development is, read the next article.

Why an e-portfolio?

The development of an e-portfolio is based on the theory that reflective practice enables students to document and track their learning and practice. It encourages students to develop an integrated, coherent picture of their learning outcomes which will now be put into practice in a Social Service placement in a real teaching-learning scenario. This process will enable you to plan and proceed towards your future goals and allow you to display your skills and knowledge to prospective employers. Electronic portfolios, as you can probably foresee, document evidence of an in-service or pre-service (trainee) teacher’s competencies and guide long-term professional development in an electronic or digital format.

Background theory supporting e-portfolios

E-portfolios are a form of personal knowledge management, supported by ICTs. Davenport and Prusak (1998) describe Personal Knowledge Management (PKM) as a set of processes to organize and integrate the information that individuals consider important, and then one can make it part of one’s personal knowledge database. They also mention that it is mandatory that workers in the age of knowledge have tools to transform random pieces of information into something systematically applicable and capable of increasing their personal knowledge.

Hutchings et al. (1992) state that "most learning seemed to occur with those who had prepared the course material rather than those who received it" (p. 171). This conclusion is consistent with constructivist theory, which argues that learners actively construct their own knowledge rather than simply receive it from instructors, authors, or other sources (Dana & Tippins, 1998; Jonassen, 1991).

Lorenzo and Ittelson (2005) say that e-portfolios tend to have seven major functions. They enable users to:

  • Plan educational programmes.
  • Document knowledge, skills, abilities, and learning.
  • Track development within a programme.
  • Define, develop and embark on a career path.
  • Evaluate a course.
  • Monitor and evaluate performance.
  • Pair social networking and informal learning with traditional classroom education.
  • And we can add number eight: help you detect real problems in real contexts and find solutions for them.

The e-portfolio has emerged as an instrument that facilitates mobility, transparency, and acknowledgement of the formal and informal learning developed throughout life (Oliveira, 2006).

What competencies can be developed with an e-portfolio?

The creation of e-portfolios in our Social Service programme pursues:

  • To foster teaching-learning strategies.
  • To track and assess your teaching.
  • To allow you to assess your own learning reflected in your teaching outcomes.
  • To work on your professional development.
  • To foster organisationial skills.
  • To cultivate thinking skills.
  • To encourage communication among you, students, peers, colleagues and faculty (or employer).
  • To use e-skills.

Portfolios are a never-ending process. Portfolios can be modified at points throughout your career, as you as a teacher continue employing learning to practice. Portfolios are probably not new to you because I am sure that you have kept a portfolio for each of your subjects and within them you have probably some tasks that you like better than others. You can select what you want to include in your portfolio, within the guidelines provided for the course.

We recommend that you include lesson plans, evaluation and self-evaluation reports, essays, reflections, your certificates, your CV, etc., and remember, because your portfolio is online, it’s possible to include photos, videos, links, and so on, whenever they are relevant to the content of your portfolio.
